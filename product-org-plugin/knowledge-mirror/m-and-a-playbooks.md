## 1. Purpose and Framing

Post-close integration is where deal value is either realized or destroyed. The committee memo is written in layers and levers; the integration team lives in workstreams, calendars, owner names, and milestones. This pack provides the scaffolding that converts the value case from `ma-value-stack` into sequenced, owned integration work. It names the eleven workstreams that run in every deal, the archetype-specific weighting that tells the integration lead which workstreams deserve the most oxygen, the five-lever decomposition that gives each workstream a verifiable synergy hunt, and the hand-off discipline that feeds diligence questions back into `/deal-diligence-checklist` (C1.1).

The playbook tells the team HOW to realize value. The value stack tells the team WHAT to realize. The playbook does NOT substitute for deal-specific judgment, does NOT predict outcomes, does NOT replace the integration management office's situational decisions, and does NOT lift content from Bain, McKinsey, BCG, PwC, EY, Deloitte, or any other M&A advisory publication. Every workstream definition, lever treatment, archetype module, and diligence question hook is authored from first principles.

## 2. Integration Workstreams

Post-close integration is run as eleven parallel workstreams. Each workstream has a primary layer responsibility (inherited from `ma-value-stack` Section 9), a typical functional lead, critical milestones at Day 1 / Day 30 / Day 100 / Year 1, and named hand-offs to other workstreams. The workstreams are constant across archetypes; what changes by archetype is the weighting (Section 4).

### 2.1 Governance

**Primary layer**: all (governance is the frame for every other workstream).
**Secondary touches**: -
**Typical lead**: Head of Corporate Development or Integration Management Office (IMO) lead.
**Day 1**: Integration Steering Committee seated; IMO stood up; charter published; decision rights (who decides what) documented; escalation path to deal committee named.
**Day 30**: Workstream leads named for all eleven workstreams; integration calendar published; weekly IMO cadence running; risk register open; tracking sheet against frozen standalone baseline active.
**Day 100**: First quarterly synergy reporting cycle against frozen baseline; decision rights reviewed and adjusted; issues escalated to Steering Committee with named owner.
**Year 1**: Annual integration retrospective; decision rights handed back to functional leadership; IMO sunset plan or transition to ongoing program management.
**Hand-offs**: feeds the strategic communications workstream (messaging to combined workforce); receives workstream status from all other workstreams for consolidated reporting.

### 2.2 People and Culture

**Primary layer**: Layer 4.
**Secondary touches**: Layer 2 (capability transfer), Layer 3 (leadership continuity underwriting strategic thesis).
**Typical lead**: Chief People Officer or Head of Integration HR.
**Day 1**: Key-person retention agreements in force; Day-1 communication to all employees; reporting structures published; "Day-1 questions" FAQ distributed.
**Day 30**: Cultural assessment baseline (pre-deal frameworks such as Denison or OCAI, applied post-close); key-person check-ins complete; retention mechanism status tracked against named individuals.
**Day 100**: First attrition read against target retention rate; culture integration plan published; known dependencies to the capability transfer workstream resolved.
**Year 1**: Retention rate reported to Steering Committee against named key-person list; culture compatibility re-assessed; talent review cycle for combined entity complete.
**Hand-offs**: feeds the capability transfer workstream (who is available to teach and who to learn); feeds governance with retention status; receives integration cost budget for retention mechanisms from the finance workstream.

### 2.3 Operations

**Primary layer**: Layer 2.
**Secondary touches**: Layer 1 (cost synergies manifest via operational consolidation), Layer 4 (process changes touch people).
**Typical lead**: Chief Operating Officer or Head of Operations.
**Day 1**: Operations continuity plan in force; single point of accountability for delivery against pre-existing customer commitments; no-disruption window defined.
**Day 30**: Process inventory complete across both entities; duplication map published; shared-services consolidation scope defined but not yet executed.
**Day 100**: First wave of process consolidation complete (typically finance back-office, HR shared services, procurement); second-wave scope and sequencing decided.
**Year 1**: Second and third consolidation waves complete; combined operating model stable; capacity utilization and throughput measured against locked baseline.
**Hand-offs**: feeds the finance workstream with cost realization data; receives people-readiness data from the people workstream before each consolidation wave; synchronizes with IT/data for systems consolidation.

### 2.4 Commercial and Revenue

**Primary layer**: Layer 1 (revenue) + Layer 2 (sales process).
**Secondary touches**: Layer 3 (strategic repositioning of combined GTM), Layer 4 (sales team retention).
**Typical lead**: Chief Revenue Officer or combined sales leadership.
**Day 1**: Customer-facing continuity plan (no surprise pricing changes, no surprise account-owner changes); combined account map drafted; customer communication plan in force.
**Day 30**: Cross-sell pilot cohort identified; combined pipeline visibility; sales incentive compatibility assessed; "no poaching of own customers" rules published.
**Day 100**: First cross-sell pilot results read; sales territory consolidation decided; combined GTM playbook drafted; incentive plan for combined sales force drafted.
**Year 1**: Cross-sell conversion rate reported against pre-deal assumption; combined GTM operating; sales cycle length benchmarked against pre-deal baselines.
**Hand-offs**: receives customer relationship data from the customer retention workstream; feeds the finance workstream with revenue realization; receives brand architecture decisions from the brand workstream.

### 2.5 Finance and Controls

**Primary layer**: Layer 1.
**Secondary touches**: Layer 5 (capital efficiency, tax structure).
**Typical lead**: Chief Financial Officer or Integration CFO.
**Day 1**: Combined chart of accounts mapped (even if not yet unified); Day-1 balance sheet snapshot; standalone baseline locked as a separate document referenced by the tracking sheet.
**Day 30**: Synergy tracking sheet live with gross and net columns, line-by-line to named integration cost; monthly close cadence for combined entity.
**Day 100**: First full quarterly close against frozen baseline; working capital harmonization scope defined; integration cost running against budget with named overruns escalated.
**Year 1**: Combined entity financial reporting at steady state; synergy realization reported against committee memo; integration cost fully run-out; capital efficiency (working capital, tax) lever realization reported.
**Hand-offs**: feeds governance with consolidated tracking; receives realization data from all value-producing workstreams; feeds strategic communications with numbers for stakeholder updates.

### 2.6 Legal and Regulatory

**Primary layer**: Layer 5 (regulatory relationships, IP).
**Secondary touches**: Layer 3 (strategic defensibility), Layer 4 (employment law continuity).
**Typical lead**: General Counsel or Integration Counsel.
**Day 1**: Change-of-control clauses in material contracts reviewed; customer notification obligations met; regulatory change-of-control filings submitted; IP assignments closed.
**Day 30**: Contract inventory complete; material contracts flagged for renegotiation, novation, or termination; regulatory approval tracking running; employment law continuity verified across jurisdictions.
**Day 100**: First wave of material contract renegotiations complete; regulatory approvals received or delayed with named mitigation; employment harmonization plan published.
**Year 1**: Contract portfolio at steady state; regulatory position stable; employment law harmonization complete across jurisdictions.
**Hand-offs**: feeds the customer retention workstream on contract-change implications; feeds the IT/data workstream on data protection and consent implications; feeds the brand workstream on trademark and licensing implications.

### 2.7 IT and Data

**Primary layer**: Layer 2 (operational systems) + Layer 5 (data assets).
**Secondary touches**: Layer 1 (IT cost consolidation).
**Typical lead**: Chief Information Officer or Integration CIO.
**Day 1**: System access continuity; no Day-1 system change that interrupts customers or employees; inventory of critical systems and dependencies.
**Day 30**: Systems inventory complete; target-state architecture drafted; data inventory and classification complete; security posture of combined entity assessed.
**Day 100**: First systems consolidation wave (typically identity, email, collaboration); data migration plan for high-value data assets published; change-of-control data consent analysis complete.
**Year 1**: Target-state architecture reached for critical systems; data assets integrated where strategic and preserved where not; IT operating cost reported against pre-deal baseline.
**Hand-offs**: receives data asset preservation requirements from the data/IP workstream; feeds the operations workstream on system-enabled process consolidation; feeds the legal workstream on data consent and regulatory implications.

### 2.8 Customer Retention

**Primary layer**: Layer 5 (customer relationship assets).
**Secondary touches**: Layer 1 (revenue continuity), Layer 3 (strategic customer signal).
**Typical lead**: Chief Customer Officer or Head of Customer Success.
**Day 1**: Top-N customer list named; account-owner continuity in force for top accounts; customer communication sent; customer-facing operational continuity verified.
**Day 30**: First read on customer sentiment (survey, executive outreach, escalation volume); top-N accounts individually checked; at-risk accounts flagged with named recovery owner.
**Day 100**: Customer churn tracked against pre-deal baseline; NPS tracked against pre-deal baseline; at-risk account recovery status reported; combined account plans for top tier complete.
**Year 1**: Customer churn reported against deal-case assumption; customer expansion from cross-sell reported; customer segment strategy for combined entity stable.
**Hand-offs**: feeds the commercial workstream on cross-sell readiness; feeds the brand workstream on customer-perception of brand architecture; receives contract-change data from the legal workstream.

### 2.9 Brand

**Primary layer**: Layer 5 (intangible, brand equity).
**Secondary touches**: Layer 3 (strategic positioning), Layer 1 (marketing OpEx).
**Typical lead**: Chief Marketing Officer or Head of Brand.
**Day 1**: Day-1 brand decision communicated (absorb, co-brand, preserve, platform); customer-facing brand continuity plan; press release; employee brand communication.
**Day 30**: Brand architecture decision finalized; trademark portfolio reviewed; marketing calendar for combined entity drafted.
**Day 100**: First brand-awareness read (if applicable); brand equity tracking started; marketing consolidation (agencies, tools, budget) complete.
**Year 1**: Brand architecture stable; marketing cost reported against pre-deal baseline; brand equity tracked against decision (preserved, absorbed, co-branded, platform).
**Hand-offs**: receives customer sentiment data from the customer retention workstream; feeds the commercial workstream on GTM messaging; receives trademark decisions from the legal workstream.

### 2.10 Data and IP

**Primary layer**: Layer 5.
**Secondary touches**: Layer 3 (strategic capability defensibility), Layer 2 (operational data re-use).
**Typical lead**: Chief Data Officer, Head of IP, or combined.
**Day 1**: IP assignment closed; patent and trademark portfolio inventory complete; data asset change-of-control analysis started.
**Day 30**: Data asset preservation plan; IP freedom-to-operate analysis; consent flow analysis for data assets; regulatory relationship transfer status.
**Day 100**: Data asset integration scope defined (where data is integrated, where it is preserved, where it is stranded); IP licensing and enforcement strategy decided; model and training data protection in force (AI-first targets).
**Year 1**: Data assets at steady state; IP portfolio managed under combined entity strategy; licensing revenue from IP monetization reported against pre-deal value case.
**Hand-offs**: feeds the IT/data workstream with technical requirements; feeds the legal workstream with IP enforcement needs; feeds the commercial workstream on IP-enabled product capabilities.

### 2.11 Strategic Communications

**Primary layer**: all (communications wraps every workstream).
**Secondary touches**: -
**Typical lead**: Chief Communications Officer or Head of Communications.
**Day 1**: Employee communication, customer communication, investor communication, press release, regulator notifications where required.
**Day 30**: Internal communication cadence running (weekly employee update from IMO or CEO); external communication cadence running; narrative consistency check against the deal committee memo.
**Day 100**: First public progress report (if the deal is material); analyst and investor updates; internal narrative refresh.
**Year 1**: Integration story told externally; lessons published internally; transition of communications cadence back to functional communications owners.
**Hand-offs**: receives status from all other workstreams; feeds governance on stakeholder sentiment; coordinates with brand on external narrative.

## 3. The 5 Levers -- Concrete Method and Diligence Hook per Lever

The load-bearing section. For each of the 5 Bain levers (inherited from `ma-value-stack` Section 3), the playbook produces (a) a concrete synergy identification method, (b) a quantification method with `[TBD]` placeholders, (c) diligence question hooks for `/deal-diligence-checklist` (C1.1), (d) a realization timeline pattern, and (e) common failure modes.

Integration test applied to every lever before publication: can this lever produce BOTH a concrete method AND a diligence hook? If not, the lever is cosmetic and gets cut or simplified. All 5 levers below pass the test.

### 3.1 Lever 1 -- Cost Synergies

**Synergy identification method.** Run a duplication map across the combined OpEx base: for every function (finance, HR, IT, legal, facilities, procurement, engineering support, customer support), list every activity, its owner, and its cost. Tag duplications where the same activity is performed twice. A duplication map is owned by the Operations workstream lead, built from headcount data (Finance + HR), contract data (Procurement + Legal), and facility data (Operations). The map is produced in the first 45 days post-close and is the basis for every cost synergy claim in the tracking sheet.

**Quantification method.** For each duplication, compute current cost on both sides, name the target-state activity location (acquirer, target, neither, shared service), compute projected cost at target state, compute gross synergy as (current - projected), compute net synergy by subtracting named integration cost (severance, retention, systems, advisory, facility exit). Gross and net are tracked in separate columns. Integration cost floor: `[TBD]` per synergy dollar, based on deal-specific analysis -- do not assume a universal ratio. Realization curve: Quarter-by-quarter, not a Year 1 lump.

**Diligence question hooks (for C1.1):**
1. **Q(L1-1)**: What is the duplication map between the target's and acquirer's cost bases by function? *Evidence*: side-by-side org charts with named roles, procurement contract inventory, facility inventory. *Archetype weighting*: dominant for Financial Buyout and Distressed; secondary for Strategic Bolt-on; minimal for AI-First.
2. **Q(L1-2)**: What integration cost has been modeled for every claimed cost synergy, and is it netted against gross? *Evidence*: synergy tracking sheet with gross and net columns, integration cost budget with named line items. *Archetype weighting*: dominant for every archetype with a Layer 1 thesis.
3. **Q(L1-3)**: Where does the cost synergy depend on reducing roles that also carry Layer 4 (human capital) value from the deal thesis? *Evidence*: cross-reference of cost-reduction roles against key-person list and capability audit. *Archetype weighting*: critical for AI-First and Strategic Platform (see D1 anti-pattern, Section 5); important for Strategic Bolt-on.
4. **Q(L1-4)**: What is the realization curve for each cost synergy (Quarter 1 / Q2-4 / Year 2+), and what is the evidentiary basis for the curve shape? *Evidence*: realization curve by synergy line, historical analogs from prior deals, pilot data. *Archetype weighting*: dominant for Financial Buyout.
5. **Q(L1-5)**: How is the standalone baseline frozen, and how is drift prevented over the integration period? *Evidence*: frozen baseline document, monthly tracking against frozen baseline, governance gate preventing baseline re-forecasting. *Archetype weighting*: universal.

**Realization timeline pattern.**
- **Q1**: Quick wins from removable duplication at the top of the org (exec support, duplicate CFO/CHRO/CIO functions, duplicate legal and audit fees). Announced synergies typically hit `[TBD]%` of target in Q1; real realization is lower because integration cost is front-loaded.
- **Q2-4**: Shared services consolidation (finance back-office, HR ops, IT service desk); procurement category renegotiation; real estate consolidation for co-located facilities. Bulk of Year-1 synergy realization happens in this window if the duplication map was produced on time.
- **Year 2+**: Systems consolidation that unlocked deeper process consolidation; facility closures that required lease expiration; long-tail procurement categories. The "second wave" is where deals that looked underperforming in Year 1 either recover or confirm as underperformers.

**Common failure modes.**
1. **Integration cost absorbs the gross synergy.** Severance, retention bonuses, systems consolidation, advisory fees, and lease exit costs consume `[TBD]%` of the gross, leaving net synergy materially below the committee memo.
2. **Scale-without-coordination.** Headcount reductions are made before the duplication map is complete, cutting roles that turn out to carry institutional knowledge or capability value.
3. **Strategic repositioning announced without execution plan** that would have changed which costs are duplicated versus strategic.
4. **Procurement savings over-promised because vendor renegotiation takes longer than the synergy curve assumed**.
5. **The D1 destructive interaction** (Section 5): cost synergy prosecution at the human capital layer destroys the Layer 4 value that was the deal thesis. This is the highest-severity failure mode for any deal whose thesis depends on retaining the target's people.

### 3.2 Lever 2 -- Revenue Synergies

**Synergy identification method.** Run a cross-sell opportunity map: for each customer in the target's base, tag whether the customer fits the acquirer's Ideal Customer Profile, whether the customer already buys a competing product to the acquirer's offering, whether the customer's buying motion is compatible with the acquirer's sales motion, and whether the customer's budget cycle allows a new purchase in the first 12 months. Apply the same analysis from the acquirer's base to the target's products. Owned by the Commercial workstream lead, built from CRM data, customer segmentation, and (if possible) a short-cycle pilot with a willing customer cohort before committing to the synergy number.

**Quantification method.** For each customer cohort, compute addressable customers x assumed conversion rate x assumed ARPU. Conversion rate MUST be an evidentiary number (pilot data, historical analog from a prior comparable deal, or explicit `[TBD]` flag for the committee), never a round percentage. ARPU is the acquirer's realized ARPU for the product, not list price. Bundle uplift is separately computed and separately tracked. Realization curve is back-loaded: Q1-2 is setup (enablement, incentive redesign, customer introductions), Q3-4 is the first pilot wave, Year 2+ is the broad rollout.

**Diligence question hooks (for C1.1):**
1. **Q(L2-1)**: What is the evidentiary basis for the cross-sell conversion rate in the committee memo? *Evidence*: pilot data, historical cross-sell rates from comparable deals, documented customer demand signals, or explicit acknowledgement that the number is unevidenced. *Archetype weighting*: critical for Strategic Bolt-on and Strategic Platform; low for AI-First and Distressed.
2. **Q(L2-2)**: Is the target's sales motion compatible with the acquirer's, or will the combined GTM require a new sales motion? *Evidence*: side-by-side sales process maps, deal-cycle length comparison, sales incentive structure comparison. *Archetype weighting*: critical for any deal with meaningful revenue synergy claims.
3. **Q(L2-3)**: Does the combined sales team have the capacity to execute the cross-sell without compromising base revenue? *Evidence*: quota capacity analysis, account coverage analysis, sales retention plan. *Archetype weighting*: critical for Strategic Bolt-on; important for Strategic Platform.
4. **Q(L2-4)**: What pricing realization is assumed post-close in consolidated categories, and what competitive response has been modeled? *Evidence*: pricing power analysis, competitive response scenarios, customer willingness-to-pay studies. *Archetype weighting*: critical for Strategic Platform and consolidation plays.
5. **Q(L2-5)**: What customer churn is assumed in the cross-sold base, and is the churn assumption backed by historical cross-sell data? *Evidence*: cohort churn analysis from prior cross-sell efforts, customer-retention baseline for the target. *Archetype weighting*: critical for any Layer 1 thesis that depends on sustained cross-sell revenue.

**Realization timeline pattern.**
- **Q1**: Setup only. Sales enablement, incentive compatibility, combined account plans for top accounts. Revenue realization in Q1 is near-zero and claiming otherwise is a pattern warning.
- **Q2-4**: First wave of cross-sell pilots with pre-identified customer cohorts. Measured against explicit conversion rate assumption; drift is surfaced early.
- **Year 2+**: Broad rollout if Year 1 pilots validated the assumption; scope-reduction or synergy re-baseline if they did not. The committee should pre-commit to a re-decision trigger (Section 7) before locking the synergy.

**Common failure modes.**
1. **Conversion rate assumed at round-number levels** (10%, 20%) with no evidentiary basis.
2. **Sales motion incompatibility surfaces late**: the acquirer's enterprise motion cannot sell the target's SMB product, or vice versa.
3. **Sales incentive plan not updated in time**, so the combined sales force has no reason to prioritize cross-sell.
4. **Target customers selected the target specifically because they did not want the acquirer's product**, making them negative cross-sell candidates.
5. **Revenue synergies absorbed by base-business attrition**: the cross-sell wins are real but masked by customer churn driven by integration friction.

### 3.3 Lever 3 -- Capability Uplift

**Synergy identification method.** Run a capability audit pre-close (as part of diligence) and re-run it at Day 30, Day 100, and Day 365. The audit names specific capabilities on each side: engineering practices, operating rhythms, tooling, proprietary methods, data science methods, customer support process maturity, regulatory relationship management, etc. For each capability, name (a) which side has it, (b) which side would benefit from it, (c) the diffusion mechanism (structural sponsorship, paired-programming, documentation, training), (d) the named owner of the diffusion on each side, and (e) the protection mechanism that prevents the source capability from degrading during the transfer window. Owned by the People workstream lead with the Operations workstream lead as co-owner. Frequently the most durable source of post-close value and frequently the hardest to name in dollars at diligence time.

**Quantification method.** Capability uplift is measured through leading indicators (adoption of practices, tooling usage, cycle time in the capability-adopting function) and lagging indicators (unit cost, throughput, quality metrics, customer-measurable outcomes). Dollar value is computed as a derived number from the lagging indicators, with `[TBD]` placeholders at diligence and `[per deal]` refinement post-close. Do not model capability uplift as a fixed-dollar synergy in Year 1; model it as a probability-weighted option with a 24-36 month realization horizon.

**Diligence question hooks (for C1.1):**
1. **Q(L3-1)**: What named capabilities are being transferred, from which side to which side? *Evidence*: capability audit with specific named practices, tools, or methods. *Archetype weighting*: critical for Strategic Bolt-on and AI-First; important for Strategic Platform.
2. **Q(L3-2)**: For each named capability, who owns the diffusion and what is the diffusion mechanism? *Evidence*: integration plan with named diffusion owners, structural sponsorship commitment, training and paired-programming schedule. *Archetype weighting*: critical whenever the capability is load-bearing.
3. **Q(L3-3)**: How is the source capability protected from degradation during the transfer window? *Evidence*: named protection mechanism (isolation of the source team from general integration friction, leadership sponsorship, working-environment commitment). *Archetype weighting*: critical for AI-First; important for Strategic Bolt-on in deep-tech categories.
4. **Q(L3-4)**: What is the measurement plan for verifying capability uplift happened (not just the diffusion activity)? *Evidence*: leading and lagging indicator set, measurement cadence, re-audit schedule. *Archetype weighting*: universal when the lever is claimed.
5. **Q(L3-5)**: Is this a genuine capability uplift or is it mis-labeled cost synergy (anti-pattern 6.2 from ma-value-stack)? *Evidence*: decomposition of the claimed synergy into the removal-of-duplication component (cost synergy) and the performance-improvement component (capability uplift). *Archetype weighting*: universal.

**Realization timeline pattern.**
- **Q1**: Capability audit complete; diffusion plan published; no measurable uplift yet.
- **Q2-4**: First cohort of capability-adopting teams in flight; leading indicators (practice adoption, tool usage, process compliance) readable; lagging indicators not yet meaningful.
- **Year 2+**: Lagging indicators (cycle time, cost, throughput, quality) reported; dollar value inferred and retro-applied to the tracking sheet; broader diffusion decisions made.

**Common failure modes.**
1. **Proximity-mistaken-for-transfer**: capability is assumed to diffuse by proximity when in practice it requires explicit structural programs.
2. **Source capability degrades during transfer** because the source team absorbs integration friction while also teaching.
3. **Capability is lost at Layer 4** because the people who carried it left (the D3 cell collapse from ma-value-stack).
4. **The transfer direction was wrong**: the acquirer imposed its process on the target's capability area, destroying the capability that was the deal rationale.
5. **Uplift is mis-labeled as cost synergy** and absorbed into a Year 1 number that then does not realize.

### 3.4 Lever 4 -- Strategic Repositioning

**Synergy identification method.** Run a three-question stress test on the strategic repositioning claim (from ma-value-stack Cell [C4]):
- (1) Would the repositioning pay the deal premium if Layer 1 synergies did not materialize?
- (2) Is there a specific, named mechanism by which the repositioning produces value?
- (3) Is the repositioning durable against realistic competitive response?

If any answer is no, the strategic thesis is probably post-hoc. For the thesis to be actionable in the playbook, it must also be decomposed into a concrete operating-model change (see the Operations workstream, Section 2.3), a concrete brand decision (see the Brand workstream, Section 2.9), and a concrete commercial execution plan (see the Commercial workstream, Section 2.4). Owned by the Integration Steering Committee lead with support from Commercial and Brand workstream leads.

**Quantification method.** Measured at Layer 3 metrics, not Layer 1 metrics: market share by a market-definition locked at signing, win-rate in named segments, pricing realization by category, platform composition rate (how often the two entities' assets are sold together), analyst recognition and buyer-perception studies. Dollar value flows from Layer 3 to Layer 1 with a lag; the committee memo must not claim Layer 1 dollar impact in Year 1 for a Layer 3 thesis unless the pricing or share mechanism has been demonstrated.

**Diligence question hooks (for C1.1):**
1. **Q(L4-1)**: Does the strategic repositioning pay the premium on its own, without Layer 1 synergies? *Evidence*: standalone-repositioning valuation, Layer 3 metric forecast, premium-decomposition analysis. *Archetype weighting*: critical for Strategic Platform; important for Strategic Bolt-on.
2. **Q(L4-2)**: What is the named mechanism (consolidation -> pricing, foothold -> adjacent revenue, capability gap -> defensibility, moat -> competitive delay, platform -> integrated offering)? *Evidence*: mechanism description with named operational ownership; concrete integration plan for the mechanism. *Archetype weighting*: critical for Strategic Platform.
3. **Q(L4-3)**: What competitive response has been modeled, and does the repositioning survive it? *Evidence*: competitor response scenarios, moat durability analysis, time-to-copy analysis. *Archetype weighting*: critical for consolidation plays.
4. **Q(L4-4)**: Is the Layer 3 thesis dependent on Layer 4 human capital (leadership continuity, cultural compatibility) that is not mechanism-backed? *Evidence*: named individuals whose departure would destroy the thesis, retention mechanisms for those individuals. *Archetype weighting*: critical for Strategic Platform (see D1 anti-pattern).
5. **Q(L4-5)**: What Layer 3 metric will be tracked post-close, and what is the pre-deal baseline against which it is measured? *Evidence*: named metric, locked definition, pre-deal baseline, measurement cadence. *Archetype weighting*: universal when Lever 4 is claimed.

**Realization timeline pattern.**
- **Q1**: Messaging and positioning launched; combined operating-model shift initiated; customer communication of the strategic repositioning.
- **Q2-4**: Layer 3 leading indicators readable (win-rate changes, pricing tests in consolidated categories, analyst coverage shifts); platform composition work underway but not yet producing revenue.
- **Year 2+**: Lagging indicators realize (market share, sustained pricing power, platform revenue); re-decision on whether the repositioning delivered against thesis.

**Common failure modes.**
1. **Strategic value as post-hoc rationalization** of a price that Layer 1 could not justify.
2. **Repositioning announced without execution plan**: the committee memo claims platform effects but the integration plan has no workstream that owns the composition.
3. **Market definition drift**: the "market" whose share is being consolidated is redrawn post-close to make the math work.
4. **Layer 4 dependency ignored**: the repositioning depends on the target's leadership team or cultural attributes, which are not retained.
5. **Competitive response erodes the moat faster than assumed**.

### 3.5 Lever 5 -- Capital Efficiency

**Synergy identification method.** Run a capital audit of the combined entity: working capital policy comparison (DSO, DPO, inventory days), non-core asset inventory, tax structure opportunity assessment, capital allocation discipline comparison. Working capital release is computed as the combined-entity target working capital ratio applied to combined revenue, versus the sum of standalone working capital ratios. Non-core asset disposal value is computed from comparable transactions. Tax structure optimization is scoped to tax counsel (out of scope for this playbook; flagged as dependency). Owned by the Finance workstream lead with Treasury and Tax support.

**Quantification method.** Working capital release in dollars, tracked against the frozen baseline from Day 1. Non-core asset disposal proceeds, net of transaction cost. Cash tax impact, tracked by tax counsel against the pre-deal structure, with clear attribution to the deal structure choices. ROIC decomposition separating operating-margin impact from capital-turnover impact. Placeholder discipline: specific dollar numbers are `[TBD]` or `[per deal]`.

**Diligence question hooks (for C1.1):**
1. **Q(L5-1)**: What is the working capital release opportunity, and what is the policy change required to realize it? *Evidence*: DSO/DPO/inventory comparison, policy harmonization plan, customer and supplier impact assessment. *Archetype weighting*: dominant for Financial Buyout; important for Distressed; secondary for Strategic Bolt-on/Platform.
2. **Q(L5-2)**: What non-core assets have been identified for disposal, and at what expected multiple? *Evidence*: asset inventory, comparable transaction analysis, disposal timeline. *Archetype weighting*: dominant for Financial Buyout and Distressed.
3. **Q(L5-3)**: Has tax counsel signed off on the tax structure, and what cash tax impact has been quantified? *Evidence*: tax counsel memo, structure diagram, cash tax forecast. *Archetype weighting*: dominant for Financial Buyout.
4. **Q(L5-4)**: Does the working capital harmonization create customer or supplier risk that could erode Layer 5 (customer relationships)? *Evidence*: customer impact analysis of DSO changes, supplier impact analysis of DPO changes, commercial escalation paths for objections. *Archetype weighting*: critical when aggressive harmonization is proposed.
5. **Q(L5-5)**: Has the capital efficiency lever been cross-referenced against archetype weighting to surface over- or under-indexing? *Evidence*: archetype-to-lever weighting map (Section 4), explicit rationale for any deviation. *Archetype weighting*: universal.

**Realization timeline pattern.**
- **Q1**: Policy harmonization decided; non-core asset list published; tax structure in force.
- **Q2-4**: Working capital release realized as receivables and payables work through the cycle; first non-core asset disposals complete; tax optimization running.
- **Year 2+**: Full working capital benefit run-rate; non-core asset disposal largely complete; tax structure steady state.

**Common failure modes.**
1. **Aggressive DSO harmonization triggers customer pushback** that erodes customer retention.
2. **Non-core asset disposal at distressed multiples** because of integration-period urgency.
3. **Tax structure decisions unwind** under regulatory challenge or change of law.
4. **Capital efficiency under-indexed in strategic deals** because the narrative focuses on Layer 3; material working capital release goes uncaptured.
5. **ROIC gains absorbed by integration cost** (severance and systems consolidation consuming the working capital release).

## 4. Archetype Playbook Modules

Each of the 5 archetypes from `ma-value-stack` Section 5 gets a dedicated module. Each module names the workstream emphasis, lever weighting, archetype-specific pitfalls, Day 1 / 30 / 100 / Year 1 milestones tailored to the archetype, and integration budget and timeline pattern.

### 4.1 Strategic Bolt-On

**Integration pattern**: fast, full absorption, 6-12 months to steady state.

**Workstream emphasis.** Operations (highest), People and Culture (high -- capability retention), Commercial (high -- cross-sell is the point), IT and Data (high -- systems consolidation is fast), Brand (usually sunsetted target brand), Governance (lightweight IMO).

**Lever weighting.** Lever 3 (Capability Uplift, dominant), Lever 2 (Revenue Synergies, important), Lever 1 (Cost Synergies, modest), Lever 4 (Strategic Repositioning, low -- the thesis is a small adjustment to existing position), Lever 5 (Capital Efficiency, low).

**Common pitfalls unique to this archetype.**
1. **Speed kills capability**: the acquirer's default integration playbook runs too fast for the target's people to absorb. The engineering team that was the acquisition rationale leaves in month 6-12.
2. **Brand sunsetting done poorly**: customers notice the target brand disappearing and read it as discontinued product, triggering churn.
3. **Cross-sell over-modeled** because the target's customer base is assumed to be an ICP fit for the acquirer's full product line.
4. **D1 destructive interaction** (Section 5) hits when cost synergy prosecution removes roles that were carrying the bolt-on capability.

**Milestones.**
- **Day 1**: Full employee communication; customer-facing continuity; brand decision announced; retention agreements in force for key engineers and sales.
- **Day 30**: Capability audit complete; operating-model consolidation decided; cross-sell pilot customer cohort selected; systems consolidation roadmap published.
- **Day 100**: Capability transfer programs in flight; first cross-sell results; operational consolidation wave 1 complete; brand rationalization complete.
- **Year 1**: Combined entity at operational steady state; capability diffusion measured; cross-sell conversion reported against pre-deal assumption.

**Integration budget pattern**: `[TBD]% of deal value` for integration cost, weighted to Q1-Q3. Retention commitments are the largest single integration cost line item.

### 4.2 Strategic Platform

**Integration pattern**: medium, operating-model redesign, 12-24 months to steady state. Target brand frequently preserved as a platform asset.

**Workstream emphasis.** Governance (highest -- the operating model needs redesign), People and Culture (highest -- leadership continuity underwrites the thesis), Brand (high -- preservation decisions), Data and IP (high -- intangible assets underwrite the platform), Strategic Communications (high -- the repositioning story), Operations (medium -- do not rush integration).

**Lever weighting.** Lever 4 (Strategic Repositioning, dominant), Lever 3 (Capability Uplift, important), Lever 2 (Revenue Synergies, important), Lever 1 (Cost Synergies, low -- premature cost prosecution destroys the thesis), Lever 5 (Capital Efficiency, secondary).

**Common pitfalls unique to this archetype.**
1. **Premature cost prosecution destroys the thesis** (D1 anti-pattern hot zone). A platform deal justified by Layer 3 and Layer 4 that then chases Layer 1 cost synergies at the Layer 4 layer is self-contradicting.
2. **Brand architecture decided too fast** before the platform thesis is understood, destroying Layer 5 intangible value.
3. **Leadership departure in Year 1** because retention mechanisms were priced for continuity but executed for cost.
4. **Platform effects asserted without composition mechanism**: nobody owns the work of actually combining the two entities' assets into a new offering.

**Milestones.**
- **Day 1**: Leadership retention commitments communicated and funded; brand preservation decision announced; customer and investor communication of the platform thesis.
- **Day 30**: Operating-model redesign underway; leadership roles in combined entity named; cultural assessment baseline complete.
- **Day 100**: First Layer 3 leading indicators (analyst recognition, win-rate shift, pricing power test); operating-model redesign at first decision gate; composition work for platform offering scoped.
- **Year 1**: Platform effects measurable in Layer 3 metrics; leadership retention at or above target; operating model stable; Year 2+ composition roadmap locked.

**Integration budget pattern**: `[TBD]% of deal value`, spread over 18-24 months. Retention and culture investment dominates. Cost synergies are a secondary output, not a primary driver.

### 4.3 Financial Buyout

**Integration pattern**: minimum integration, performance improvement focus, exit planning from Day 1. 3-7 year hold horizon shapes every decision.

**Workstream emphasis.** Finance and Controls (highest), Operations (high -- margin expansion), Governance (high -- reporting discipline for the sponsor), Legal and Regulatory (high -- contract and tax structure), Customer Retention (medium -- stability, not expansion), People and Culture (medium -- management equity and retention, not cultural integration).

**Lever weighting.** Lever 1 (Cost Synergies, dominant), Lever 5 (Capital Efficiency, dominant), Lever 2 (Revenue Synergies, secondary -- growth story for exit), Lever 3 (Capability Uplift, low), Lever 4 (Strategic Repositioning, secondary -- multiple expansion at exit).

**Common pitfalls unique to this archetype.**
1. **Cost reductions cut into growth capacity** that was supposed to support multiple expansion at exit.
2. **Working capital harmonization triggers customer churn** that damages the exit story.
3. **Management team departure** before the exit window because retention mechanisms were not aligned with exit horizon.
4. **Tax structure assumptions unwind** at exit because regulatory or accounting treatment changes.
5. **Anti-pattern 6.8 (base-rate drift)** is highest-risk because the hold period is long enough that the standalone baseline can drift invisibly.

**Milestones.**
- **Day 1**: Reporting cadence to sponsor in force; management equity and retention structured; frozen baseline locked.
- **Day 30**: 100-day performance improvement plan published; cost reduction targets by function; working capital harmonization plan.
- **Day 100**: First performance improvement read; non-core asset disposal list; management scorecard in operation.
- **Year 1**: Margin expansion against locked baseline; capital efficiency metrics reported; exit readiness review.

**Integration budget pattern**: `[TBD]% of deal value`, front-loaded in Year 1 for restructuring. Retention cost for management is a separate line item not in the integration budget.

### 4.4 AI-First Target

**Integration pattern**: minimum operational integration, maximum talent and IP preservation. The target is frequently maintained as a standalone unit for 12-24 months to protect the capability and team.

**Workstream emphasis.** People and Culture (highest -- the team IS the asset), Data and IP (highest -- models, training data, regulatory position), Legal and Regulatory (high -- IP, consents, regulatory change-of-control), Strategic Communications (high -- internal narrative to the team), Governance (medium -- lighter hand than a strategic bolt-on).

**Lever weighting.** Lever 3 (Capability Uplift, dominant -- via Cell [D3] from ma-value-stack), Lever 4 (Strategic Repositioning, important -- the acquirer's AI strategy), Lever 2 (Revenue Synergies, secondary -- IP-enabled products), Lever 1 (Cost Synergies, near-zero and actively avoided), Lever 5 (Capital Efficiency, minimal).

**Common pitfalls unique to this archetype.**
1. **Retention assumed without mechanism** (Anti-pattern 6.4 from ma-value-stack). The team leaves in months 6-18 because the acquirer's environment is incompatible.
2. **D1 destructive interaction** (Section 5) catastrophically so: any cost synergy prosecution at the human capital layer destroys the deal. This archetype is the clearest case where cost synergies MUST be off the table.
3. **IP and data consent breakage**: training data was collected under consents that do not survive change of control, stranding the capability.
4. **Regulatory relationship non-transfer**: the regulatory approvals or relationships that underwrote the thesis do not survive the change of control.
5. **Capability uplift modeled as Layer 2 instead of Layer 4**: the pack's Cell [E3] framing (intangibles as capability enablers -- data for training, regulatory relationships for approval acceleration) is not followed; the capability is treated as transferable process rather than as a human-capital-and-intangible composite.

**AI-first integration framing from Cell [E3]**. The target's data assets and regulatory relationships are not just passive intangibles; they are the enablers of the capability uplift that the acquirer is buying. The playbook must treat data assets as training infrastructure (with change-of-control consent analysis), regulatory relationships as approval-acceleration infrastructure (with transferability analysis), and the team as the operating layer that turns these intangibles into deployed capability. All three must be preserved together; losing any one collapses the deal.

**Milestones.**
- **Day 1**: Full retention package in force for named individuals; working-environment commitments communicated; team reporting structure preserved; IP assignments closed; data consent analysis started.
- **Day 30**: Team continuity survey; retention mechanism effectiveness read; data consent analysis complete; regulatory transfer status; standalone unit governance in force.
- **Day 100**: First capability audit post-close (has the team produced? are the models still advancing?); IP and data asset preservation complete; first deployed capability in acquirer's product (if applicable).
- **Year 1**: Team retention at or above target (named individuals, not aggregate); capability deployed into acquirer's operating base; IP and regulatory position stable; standalone-unit-vs-integrate decision reviewed.

**Integration budget pattern**: `[TBD]% of deal value`, but the line items are different: retention mechanisms are the largest cost, not severance or systems. The absence of a large integration cost is a feature, not a bug.

### 4.5 Distressed

**Integration pattern**: triage-first, stop-the-bleed operations, restructuring in Year 1, stabilization in Year 2. Fast but not reckless.

**Workstream emphasis.** Operations (highest -- stabilization), Finance and Controls (highest -- cash management), Legal and Regulatory (high -- restructuring and creditor management), Customer Retention (high -- at-risk accounts), People and Culture (high but selective -- who stays through the restructuring).

**Lever weighting.** Lever 1 (Cost Synergies, dominant -- stabilization), Lever 5 (Capital Efficiency, dominant -- working capital release is often the difference between recovery and failure), Lever 3 (Capability Uplift, secondary -- restoring operating capability), Lever 4 (Strategic Repositioning, secondary or later-stage), Lever 2 (Revenue Synergies, deferred -- do not overreach during stabilization).

**Common pitfalls unique to this archetype.**
1. **Cost cuts go too deep** and destroy the operational capability needed for recovery.
2. **Working capital squeeze** on creditors or suppliers ruptures the supply chain.
3. **Customer churn during the turnaround** because the deal team prioritized internal stabilization over customer continuity.
4. **Key people flight** because they read the restructuring as discontinuity.
5. **Anti-pattern 6.7 (realization speed overestimation)** hits hard: stabilization takes longer than the committee assumed and cash runs out before recovery.

**Milestones.**
- **Day 1**: Cash position assessment; critical creditor and supplier outreach; employee communication of stability; customer communication of continuity.
- **Day 30**: 100-day stabilization plan; restructuring scope; at-risk customer list; key-person retention for recovery-critical roles.
- **Day 100**: Cash-burn stabilization measured; first customer retention read; first operational stabilization read; restructuring actions initiated.
- **Year 1**: Stabilization achieved; restructuring complete; first look at upside levers (Layer 3 if any).

**Integration budget pattern**: `[TBD]% of deal value`, but the ratio to deal value is high because the value is in recovery, not in net synergies. Cash reserve for stabilization is the largest line item.

## 5. The D1 Destructive-Interaction Anti-Pattern

This is the inheritance point from `ma-value-stack` anti-pattern 6.4 and Cell [D1]. The D1 cell (Human Capital x Cost Synergies) is treated as a DESTRUCTIVE interaction, not a positive synergy. A deal whose thesis depends on human capital retention (Layer 4) but whose integration is being driven by cost synergies (Lever 1 at the Layer 4 intersection) will destroy the thesis. This section provides detection, early-warning signals, mitigation, and escalation.

### 5.1 What the Anti-Pattern Looks Like

The deal committee memo claims Layer 4 value: "we're acquiring the team," "the founder's customer relationships are load-bearing," "the engineering culture is the capability we want." The integration plan then prosecutes Layer 1 cost synergies at the same layer: "we'll consolidate duplicate engineering management," "we'll harmonize sales incentives downward to the acquirer's standard," "we'll rationalize the target's HR and finance functions into our shared services."

The contradiction is that the thesis said "these people are why we're doing this deal" and the plan says "these people are a cost to remove." Both cannot be true. In deals where the anti-pattern runs unchecked, the Layer 4 value collapses: the key people leave, the cultural attributes evaporate, the founder's customer relationships are broken, and the premium paid for the team is written off within 18-24 months.

### 5.2 Named Early-Warning Signals

The integration team should treat any of the following as an early-warning signal that the D1 anti-pattern is activating. All are observable within the first 100 days and most are observable in the first 30.

1. **CHRO or equivalent excluded from the Integration Steering Committee**. Integration governance is framed as an operational and financial exercise with HR in a supporting role. Layer 4 risk has no voice at the decision table.
2. **Retention bonuses proposed but not funded** in the integration budget. The committee memo says "we will offer retention," and the integration budget does not have a line item for it.
3. **Reduction-in-force announcements before culture work begins**. The sequence is RIF first, culture assessment later. The target's people learn from the RIF announcement that the retention language in the committee memo was not real.
4. **Target leadership excluded from the combined-entity operating model design**. The operating model is designed by the acquirer's team and presented to the target's leadership for implementation. Target leaders read this as a signal that their roles are being scoped out.
5. **Cost synergy targets announced to the Steering Committee before the duplication map is complete**. Targets become commitments; commitments become pressure; pressure leads to cuts that destroy Layer 4 value.
6. **Cross-functional retention check-ins replaced by aggregated attrition metrics**. The committee reads "4% attrition this quarter" and concludes the retention plan is working, while the aggregate masks that three of the ten named key people have left.
7. **Severance policy harmonized to the lower of the two entities' policies** as a cost-saving measure, signaling to the target's people that their employment terms have been unilaterally downgraded.
8. **Day-1 brand decisions that sunset the target brand** when the Layer 4 thesis depends partly on the target's cultural identity, which the brand carries.
9. **Budget pressure in Quarter 2** driven by a Year 1 cost synergy target that was set before the duplication map was complete.
10. **"Integration fatigue" surfacing in engagement surveys** among the target's people, while the acquirer's leadership attributes it to normal integration friction rather than Layer 4 value destruction.

### 5.3 Mitigation Playbook

When the anti-pattern is detected, the integration lead runs the following mitigation sequence:

1. **Freeze cost synergy prosecution at the Layer 4 intersection**. No further headcount reductions, no severance decisions, no role eliminations, no incentive-structure harmonization pending re-validation of the Layer 4 thesis.
2. **Reconvene the Integration Steering Committee with CHRO at the table** if they were not already. Reframe the Layer 4 value case as a primary deal rationale, not a supporting element.
3. **Re-audit the key-person list against the attrition data**. For every key person still in the company, verify the retention mechanism is in force, funded, and working. For every key person who has left, assess the damage to the Layer 4 thesis and escalate to the deal committee if material.
4. **Re-examine the cost synergy target**. Distinguish between (a) duplicated roles where the capability is not scarce and the cost reduction is genuinely non-destructive, and (b) roles where the cost synergy destroys Layer 4 value. Reduce the cost synergy target by the Layer-4-destructive portion and re-baseline with the committee.
5. **Re-communicate to the target's people**. The internal communication should explicitly acknowledge the course correction, name the retention commitment, and re-affirm the Layer 4 thesis in plain language.
6. **Re-examine the operating model for the combined entity**. If the operating model was designed without the target's leadership input, re-open the design with target leadership at the table.
7. **Run a Layer 4 recovery read at Day 30 after mitigation** and at Day 100. If the Layer 4 thesis cannot be recovered, escalate to re-decision (Section 7).

### 5.4 Escalation Triggers

The integration lead escalates to the deal committee when any of the following are observed post-mitigation:

1. **Attrition of named key people above `[TBD]%` of the pre-close key-person list** within the first 12 months.
2. **Cultural assessment re-read showing material erosion** of the cultural attributes named in the deal memo as part of the Layer 4 thesis.
3. **Commercial metrics from the target's customer base showing relationship erosion** (churn, NPS, account health) traceable to loss of relationship owners.
4. **Leadership team instability** in the target's leadership tier, especially in the first 12 months.
5. **Evidence that the Layer 4 thesis cannot be recovered** even with re-committed retention mechanisms, triggering a re-decision on the deal's Layer 1 floor alone.

### 5.5 Why This Section Is Load-Bearing

The D1 destructive-interaction framing is the inheritance point from `ma-value-stack` v1.0.0. If the playbook does not treat D1 as destructive, the playbook is inconsistent with the value stack and the system is internally contradictory. This section exists to make the inconsistency impossible: any integration team consuming the playbook is explicitly told that cost synergy prosecution at the human capital layer is DESTRUCTIVE to deals with a Layer 4 thesis and must be governed by the early-warning and mitigation sequence above.
