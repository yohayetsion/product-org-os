## Brand Review Framework

**When to use**: Auditing brand consistency, assessing brand perception, or diagnosing gaps between intended and actual positioning.

### Brand Consistency Audit

**When to run**: Annually, or before a rebrand, major launch, or new market entry.

**Audit scope:**

| Touchpoint | Elements to Audit | Owner |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| **Website** | Homepage headline, value prop, tone, visual consistency | Marketing |
| **Sales materials** | Pitch decks, one-pagers, battle cards | Sales + Marketing |
| **Email communications** | Subject line tone, copy style, visual template | Email / Marketing Ops |
| **Social profiles** | Bio text, handle, header images, posting tone | Social Media |
| **Paid ads** | Headline, CTA, visual, landing page alignment | Paid Media |
| **PR / Press releases** | Company boilerplate, spokesperson quotes | PR / Comms |
| **Events** | Booth messaging, slides, swag | Events + Brand |
| **Product / In-app** | Onboarding copy, empty states, error messages | Product + Marketing |
| **Partner materials** | Co-branded assets, integration descriptions | BizDev + Marketing |

**Consistency scoring:**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 — Excellent | Message, tone, and visual are perfectly consistent with brand standards |
| 4 — Good | Minor variations that don't confuse or misrepresent the brand |
| 3 — Needs Work | Inconsistencies visible to a careful reader; brand feels fragmented |
| 2 — Poor | Significant deviations; different messages on different channels |
| 1 — Critical | Contradictory or outdated positioning; actively damaging |

---

### Brand Perception Analysis

**How to assess perception vs. intention:**

| Method | What It Reveals | Frequency |
|--------|----------------|-----------|
| **Brand survey** | Aided/unaided awareness, association mapping, attribute ratings | Annual |
| **Customer interviews** | How buyers describe you in their own words (vs. how you describe yourself) | Quarterly |
| **Review mining (G2/Capterra)** | How customers talk about you publicly | Monthly |
| **Sales call listening** | Language buyers use when describing problems and alternatives | Ongoing |
| **Competitor analysis** | How competitors position against you (reveals their perception of your brand) | Quarterly |
| **NPS comments** | Verbatim sentiment from your customer base | Ongoing |

**Perception gap analysis template:**

```
Intended brand perception: [How we want to be known]
Actual brand perception (evidenced): [What customers/market actually say]
Gap: [Where intended ≠ actual]

Gap analysis:
  - Is this a messaging problem? (We're not saying it clearly enough)
  - Is this a product problem? (The reality doesn't match the claim)
  - Is this a channel problem? (We're not reaching the right audience)
  - Is this a consistency problem? (Mixed signals across touchpoints)

Recommended action: [Specific changes to close the gap]
```

### Competitive Positioning Review

**When to use**: Ensuring your brand is differentiated from competitors in the market's mind, not just on your positioning doc.

**Positioning differentiation test:**

```
For each key differentiator you claim:
  1. Is it TRUE? (Can you prove it with evidence?)
  2. Is it RELEVANT? (Does your ICP actually care about this?)
  3. Is it DISTINCT? (Does a competitor claim the same thing?)
  4. Is it SUSTAINABLE? (Can you defend it over 12-24 months?)

If the answer to any of the four is NO — that differentiator is weak.
A strong differentiator passes all four.
```

**Positioning map (2x2):**

Choose two dimensions that matter most to your ICP. Plot yourself and top 3-5 competitors. Ideal position: quadrant with high scores on both dimensions where no competitor cluster exists.

### Attribution Models

**When to use**: Deciding how to credit channels for pipeline and revenue. Attribution model choice significantly affects budget allocation decisions.

| Model | How It Works | Best For | Limitation |
|-------|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **First Touch** | 100% credit to the first channel that brought the lead | Understanding awareness drivers | Ignores the nurture journey |
| **Last Touch** | 100% credit to the last touchpoint before conversion | Understanding what closes | Ignores awareness investment |
| **Linear** | Equal credit across all touchpoints | Balanced view of the full journey | Treats all touches as equal |
| **Time Decay** | More credit to touches closer to conversion | B2B long sales cycles | Under-credits top-of-funnel |
| **U-Shaped (Position-Based)** | 40% first touch, 40% last touch, 20% split across middle | B2B with long nurture | Arbitrary weighting |
| **Data-Driven** | ML assigns credit based on actual conversion patterns | Companies with large datasets | Requires significant data volume |

**Practical recommendation for most B2B companies:**
- Use **U-shaped** as primary model (balances awareness and conversion credit)
- Supplement with **first touch** to inform top-of-funnel investment
- Use **last touch** for sales team attribution and short-cycle products

## Channel Strategy Matrix

**When to use**: Deciding which channels to invest in based on funnel stage and content/format type.

| Stage | Goal | Owned Channels | Earned Channels | Paid Channels |
|-------|------|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Awareness** | Generate interest in the category or problem | Blog (SEO), social posts, podcast | PR, backlinks, word of mouth | Paid social (brand), display, content syndication |
| **Consideration** | Demonstrate fit and differentiation | Email nurture, case studies, webinars | Reviews (G2, Capterra), analyst relations | Retargeting, paid search (branded + comparison), LinkedIn Sponsored Content |
| **Decision** | Overcome final objections, create urgency | Trial, demos, pricing page, sales enablement | Customer references, peer recommendations | Retargeting (high-intent), branded search |
| **Retention** | Drive adoption, expansion, advocacy | In-product comms, customer email, community | Customer reviews, referrals | None (retention should not require paid) |

**Channel investment sequencing:**

```
Stage 1 — Foundation (Months 0-6):
  - Nail owned: website, email, core content
  - Test 1-2 paid channels with learning budgets
  - Build SEO foundation

Stage 2 — Scale proven channels (Months 6-18):
  - Double down on channels with proven CPL/CAC
  - Add earned channels (PR, reviews, partnerships)
  - Build content engine for organic compounding

Stage 3 — Expand and diversify (Months 18+):
  - Enter new channels with learnings from Stage 2
  - Invest in brand for long-term awareness compounding
  - Optimize attribution across multi-channel mix
```

## Marketing Budget Allocation Models

### The 70/20/10 Model

**When to use**: Balancing investment in proven vs. experimental channels.

| Allocation | Use | Example |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| **70% — Core** | Proven channels with predictable ROI | Paid search, email nurture, content SEO |
| **20% — Emerging** | Channels showing promise but not yet optimized | LinkedIn ABM, new ad formats, community |
| **10% — Experimental** | Bets on new channels or radical creative | New social platforms, influencer, podcast ads |

**When to rebalance:** Move a channel from Experimental → Emerging when CAC is within 2x of proven channels. Move Emerging → Core when CAC matches or beats core channels with confidence over 3+ months.

---

### Objective-Based Budget Allocation

**When to use**: Building a budget from scratch based on growth targets rather than historical spending.

```
Step 1 — Define revenue target
  Target new ARR: [$X]

Step 2 — Work backwards from revenue
  Win rate (SQL → Close): [X%]
  → SQLs needed: [Target ARR / ACV / win rate]

  MQL → SQL conversion rate: [X%]
  → MQLs needed: [SQLs needed / conversion rate]

Step 3 — Calculate cost per MQL by channel
  Channel A: CPL = [$X], MQL rate = [X%] → CPMQL = [$X]
  Channel B: CPL = [$X], MQL rate = [X%] → CPMQL = [$X]

Step 4 — Allocate budget to meet MQL target
  Total budget required = MQLs needed × blended CPMQL

Step 5 — Add brand / content overhead
  Brand and content (non-demand gen): Typically 20-30% of total budget
```

---

### Historical-Based Allocation

**When to use**: When you have 12+ months of performance data and want to optimize an existing budget.

```
Step 1 — Pull last 12 months: spend, MQLs, pipeline, and revenue by channel
Step 2 — Calculate efficiency per channel: Revenue / Spend = ROAS; Pipeline / Spend = pipeline multiplier
Step 3 — Rank channels by efficiency
Step 4 — Reallocate from low-efficiency channels to high-efficiency channels
Step 5 — Set minimum threshold: Never drop a channel below the minimum viable spend needed to generate reliable signal
```

**Common allocation benchmarks (B2B SaaS, directional):**

| Function | % of Marketing Budget |
|----------|----------------------|
| Demand generation (paid channels) | 35-50% |
| Content and SEO | 15-25% |
| Events and field marketing | 10-20% |
| Brand and PR | 5-15% |
| Marketing tools and operations | 5-10% |
| Headcount | Not included above — separate from program budget |

## Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) Models

**When to use**: Evaluating channel efficiency, setting budget targets, and modeling unit economics.

### Blended vs. Channel CAC

| CAC Type | Formula | Use Case |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Blended CAC** | Total marketing + sales spend / New customers acquired | Board reporting, high-level unit economics |
| **Marketing CAC** | Marketing spend only / Marketing-sourced new customers | Marketing efficiency measurement |
| **Channel CAC** | Spend on channel X / New customers attributed to channel X | Channel optimization, budget reallocation |
| **Payback period** | CAC / Monthly gross margin per customer | Cash flow planning, fundraising |

**CAC health benchmarks (B2B SaaS):**

| CAC Payback Period | Assessment |
|-------------------|------------|
| < 12 months | Healthy — aggressive growth is justified |
| 12-18 months | Acceptable — standard for mid-market SaaS |
| 18-24 months | Concerning — review efficiency or raise prices |
| > 24 months | Problematic — fix before scaling spend |

**LTV:CAC ratio:**

| Ratio | Assessment |
|-------|------------|
| < 1:1 | Burning cash on every customer acquired |
| 1:1 — 3:1 | Marginal; improving but not yet healthy |
| 3:1 — 5:1 | Healthy SaaS benchmark |
| > 5:1 | May indicate underinvestment in growth |

**Limitations**: CAC calculations are only as accurate as your attribution model. Blended CAC often understates the true cost of a specific growth initiative. Always accompany CAC with payback period and LTV:CAC to get the full picture.

## Marketing OKR Templates

**When to use**: Setting quarterly marketing goals that are measurable and aligned to business outcomes.

### OKR Structure for Marketing

```
MARKETING OKRs — [Quarter / Year]

OBJECTIVE 1: [Qualitative aspirational goal — e.g., "Become the go-to resource for [category]"]
  KR 1.1: [Metric] from [baseline] to [target] by [date]
  KR 1.2: [Metric] from [baseline] to [target] by [date]
  KR 1.3: [Metric] from [baseline] to [target] by [date]

OBJECTIVE 2: [e.g., "Build a predictable demand engine for the sales team"]
  KR 2.1: Generate [N] MQLs at blended CPMQL of [$X] or less
  KR 2.2: Increase MQL → SQL conversion rate from [X%] to [Y%]
  KR 2.3: Launch [N] new demand gen experiments; validate [M] of them

OBJECTIVE 3: [e.g., "Strengthen brand differentiation in the enterprise segment"]
  KR 3.1: Publish [N] enterprise-focused case studies
  KR 3.2: Increase brand awareness among [ICP] from [X%] to [Y%] (survey-measured)
  KR 3.3: Achieve [N] enterprise press placements in [target publications]
```

**OKR quality checklist:**
- [ ] Objectives are inspirational — they describe a desired state, not a task
- [ ] Key Results are measurable — there is no ambiguity about whether you hit them
- [ ] Key Results are outcomes, not outputs ("increase MQLs", not "launch campaign")
- [ ] 60-70% attainment expected — if 100% likely, the goal is too easy
- [ ] Each KR has a clear owner
- [ ] OKRs connect to a business-level outcome

## Integrated Campaign Checklist

**When to use**: Before launching any multi-channel campaign. Ensures all elements are in place and aligned.

### Pre-Launch Checklist

**Strategy:**
- [ ] Campaign objective is defined and measurable
- [ ] Target audience is specific (not "everyone")
- [ ] Core message is clear and consistent across all assets
- [ ] Campaign is linked to a business goal with measurable KPIs

**Assets:**
- [ ] Landing page is live and tested (mobile + desktop)
- [ ] All ad creative is approved and uploaded
- [ ] Email sequence is built, tested, and scheduled
- [ ] Blog or content assets are published and indexed
- [ ] Social posts are written and scheduled

**Tracking:**
- [ ] UTM parameters applied to all external links
- [ ] Conversion tracking confirmed on landing page
- [ ] CRM integration tested (leads flowing to correct pipeline stage)
- [ ] Attribution model documented for this campaign

**Compliance:**
- [ ] Email list segmentation is GDPR/CAN-SPAM compliant
- [ ] Unsubscribe mechanism working in all emails
- [ ] Ad claims are truthful and substantiated
- [ ] Any claims about competitors are factual

**Team alignment:**
- [ ] Sales team briefed on campaign messaging and expected lead volume
- [ ] SDRs have follow-up scripts aligned to campaign offer
- [ ] Customer Success notified if campaign targets existing customers

### Mid-Campaign Review (At 25-50% of timeline)

- [ ] Are early metrics tracking toward the KPI target?
- [ ] Is any channel over/under-performing significantly?
- [ ] Are there creative fatigue signals? (CTR declining on ads after 7-14 days)
- [ ] Is there a budget pacing issue? (Under-spending = reach gap; over-spending = early exhaustion)
- [ ] Any compliance or PR issues surfaced?

## Marketing Tech Stack Evaluation Framework

**When to use**: Evaluating new marketing tools, consolidating existing stack, or auditing tech debt.

### Evaluation Criteria

| Criterion | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Functional fit** | High | Does it do what we need it to do? |
| **Integration** | High | Does it connect cleanly with our CRM and existing stack? |
| **Ease of use** | Medium | Can our team actually use it without heavy training? |
| **Scalability** | Medium | Will it handle 3-5x our current data volume and team size? |
| **Total cost** | Medium | Licensing + implementation + ongoing ops cost |
| **Support / vendor stability** | Low-Medium | Is the vendor reliable and well-supported? |
| **Data privacy compliance** | High | Does it meet GDPR, CCPA, and other applicable requirements? |

### Stack Audit Questions

```
For each tool in the current stack, answer:
  1. Is it actively used? (Not just licensed — actually used by >50% of the team)
  2. Does it integrate with the rest of the stack or create data silos?
  3. Is there overlap with another tool? (Could one tool replace two?)
  4. Does the cost justify the usage and outcome?
  5. Is it the best available option for its function, or just what we've always had?
```

### Marketing Tech Stack by Function

| Function | Category | Example Tools |
|----------|---------|--------------|
| **CRM / Pipeline** | Core infrastructure | Salesforce, HubSpot, Pipedrive |
| **Marketing automation** | Email + workflows | HubSpot, Marketo, Pardot, Customer.io |
| **Analytics** | Web + product | GA4, Amplitude, Mixpanel, Heap |
| **Paid media** | Ad management | Google Ads, Meta Ads, LinkedIn Campaign Manager |
| **SEO** | Organic search | Ahrefs, SEMrush, Screaming Frog |
| **CRO** | Conversion testing | VWO, Optimizely, Convert |
| **Content** | Creation + management | Contentful, WordPress, Notion, Webflow |
| **Social** | Publishing + listening | Sprout Social, Buffer, Hootsuite, Brandwatch |
| **Events / Webinars** | Virtual events | Webex, Hopin, Zoom Events, ON24 |

| **Review management** | Social proof | G2, Trustpilot, Birdeye |
| **ABM** | Account-based | 6sense, Demandbase, Terminus |

**Stack evaluation principle:** Fewer tools used well beats many tools used poorly. Before adding a new tool, exhaust what your current stack can do.
