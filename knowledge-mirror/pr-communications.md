# PR & Thought Leadership Frameworks

Knowledge pack for PR and thought leadership agents (PR/Comms Specialist, Market Researcher). Contains real frameworks for media relations, press releases, thought leadership, crisis communications, and analyst relations.

---

## Press Release Framework

### Standard Press Release Structure

**When to use**: Announcing newsworthy company developments — funding, product launches, partnerships, executive hires, awards.

**Newsworthiness test (ask before writing):**
- Would a journalist at a target publication care about this?
- Is there a genuine "so what?" for the publication's audience?
- Does this contain new information not previously available?

If the answer to any of these is no, consider a blog post or social announcement instead.

**Structure:**

```
[CITY, STATE] — [DATE] — [Opening paragraph: Who, What, When, Where, Why]

[Second paragraph: Supporting details, context, significance]

"[Executive quote — forward-looking, visionary, not self-congratulatory]" said
[Name], [Title] at [Company].

[Third paragraph: How it works, technical details, specifics]

"[Customer/partner quote — endorsement with specific value received]" said
[Name], [Title] at [Customer/Partner Company].

[Fourth paragraph: Availability, pricing, timing, next steps]

### About [Company]
[Boilerplate — 2-3 sentences. What the company does, for whom, key differentiator.]

### Media Contact
[Name] | [Email] | [Phone]
```

**Style rules:**
- Lead with the most newsworthy fact
- Keep paragraphs to 2-3 sentences
- Include at least two quotes (executive + external)
- Avoid superlatives ("leading", "best-in-class", "revolutionary") unless verifiable
- Include specific numbers wherever possible
- Write in third person

**Limitations**: Press releases alone do not generate coverage. They are a starting point. The pitch email, journalist relationship, and timing matter more than the release itself.

## Media Pitch Framework

### Crafting a Journalist Pitch

**When to use**: Reaching out to journalists for coverage — proactive pitching, exclusive offers, expert commentary.

**Pitch structure (email):**

```
Subject: [Specific, not clickbait — journalist should know the story from the subject]

Hi [First name],

[Opening: 1-2 sentences showing you read their recent work and why your story
is relevant to their beat. Be specific.]

[Pitch: 2-3 sentences describing the story angle. What is new, why it matters
to their audience, what makes it timely.]

[Offer: What you can provide — exclusive access, data, executive interview,
customer reference. Be specific about availability.]

[Close: Would this be of interest? Happy to share [specific asset].
No pressure if it's not a fit.]

[Signature]
```

**Pitch personalization checklist:**
- [ ] Addressed to specific journalist (never "Dear Editor")
- [ ] References their recent relevant work
- [ ] Explains why this story fits their beat
- [ ] Offers something specific (data, interview, exclusive)
- [ ] Short — under 200 words

**Timing:**
- Best: Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10am journalist's local time
- Avoid: Monday morning (inbox overload), Friday afternoon (weekend brain)
- Follow up: Once, 3-5 business days later. No more than two total touchpoints.

**Limitations**: Journalists receive hundreds of pitches daily. Even well-crafted pitches get ignored. Success rate of 5-15% is normal. Do not take non-response personally. Build relationships over time through consistent, valuable outreach.

## Thought Leadership Ladder

### Building Executive Authority

**When to use**: Developing a thought leadership program for executives or the company. Long-term reputation building.

**The ladder (bottom to top):**

| Level | Activity | Audience Reach | Credibility Signal |
|-------|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **1. Company blog** | Publish on owned channels | Low (existing audience) | Starting point — easy to do |
| **2. Guest posts** | Write for industry publications | Medium (publication audience) | Editor-vetted content |
| **3. Speaking** | Conference presentations, panels | Medium (event audience) | Selection committee vetted |
| **4. Bylined articles** | Published in major trade/business press | High (publication audience) | Strong editorial validation |
| **5. Quoted expert** | Journalists seek your commentary | High (news audience) | Third-party credibility |
| **6. Book/research** | Published original research or book | Very high (industry-wide) | Highest credibility signal |

### Thought Leadership Content Principles

| Principle | What It Means | Example |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Genuinely useful** | Reader learns something independent of your product | "Here are 5 questions every CISO should ask about insider risk" |
| **Evidence-based** | Claims supported by data, experience, or research | "Our analysis of 200 insider incidents revealed..." |
| **Contrarian or novel** | Says something different from consensus | "Why DLP alone creates a false sense of security" |
| **Personal experience** | Draws from real-world experience, not theory | "When I led security at [company], we learned..." |

**What thought leadership is NOT:**
- Product announcements disguised as insights
- Thinly veiled sales pitches
- Restating industry consensus without adding perspective
- Generic advice anyone could give

**Limitations**: Thought leadership compounds over time but has no short-term ROI. It takes 6-12 months of consistent publication to build recognizable authority. Organizations expecting immediate pipeline from thought leadership will be disappointed.

## Speaking Engagement Strategy

### Getting On Stage

**When to use**: Building a speaking presence at industry events.

**Submission template (CFP — Call for Papers):**

```
Title: [Provocative or intriguing — NOT a product pitch]

Abstract (150-300 words):
[Opening hook — surprising statistic, provocative question, or bold claim]

[What the audience will learn — 3 specific takeaways]

[Why this matters now — timeliness, urgency, relevance]

[Speaker credibility — specific experience that qualifies you]

Speaker Bio (50-100 words):
[Name] is [Title] at [Company]. [Relevant experience]. [Notable achievement
or publication]. [Optional: personal detail that humanizes.]

Session Format: [Keynote / Panel / Workshop / Lightning Talk]
Target Audience: [Role, experience level]
```

**Event prioritization:**

| Criterion | Weight | Score 1-5 |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Target audience alignment | 30% | |
| Event reputation/quality | 25% | |
| Geographic relevance | 15% | |
| Networking opportunity | 15% | |
| Cost (time + travel) | 15% | |

**Limitations**: Conference speaking requires significant time investment. One 30-minute talk typically requires 20-40 hours of preparation. Prioritize ruthlessly — 3-4 well-chosen events per year beats 10 mediocre ones.

## Analyst Relations

### Working with Industry Analysts

**When to use**: When analysts cover your market category (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, etc.) and influence buyer decisions.

**Analyst engagement types:**

| Engagement | Purpose | Frequency |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Briefing** | Inform analyst about product/company updates | Quarterly or at major milestones |
| **Inquiry** | Ask analyst for advice or market perspective | As needed (often included in subscription) |
| **Evaluation** | Participate in Magic Quadrant, Wave, etc. | Annual (when invited or qualified) |
| **Strategy day** | Deep dive on company strategy and roadmap | Annual |

**Briefing preparation template:**

```
1. Company overview (2 min)
   - What you do, for whom, key differentiator

2. Market context (3 min)
   - Your view of the market, trends, drivers

3. Product update (10 min)
   - What's new, why it matters, customer impact
   - Demo if relevant

4. Customer evidence (5 min)
   - 2-3 customer examples with specific outcomes

5. Roadmap (5 min)
   - Where you're heading and why

6. Q&A (remaining time)
```

**Rules of engagement:**
- Analysts value candor over polish
- Acknowledge weaknesses — they will find them anyway
- Provide customer references who will speak honestly
- Follow up with any promised materials within 48 hours
- Never ask an analyst to change their assessment

**Limitations**: Analyst relations require subscription investment (Gartner, Forrester) and significant time. For early-stage companies, the ROI may not justify the cost until the company reaches the stage where enterprise buyers rely on analyst guidance.

## Crisis Communication Framework

### Preparing for and Responding to Crisis

**When to use**: Before a crisis (preparation) and during a crisis (response). The preparation is more important than the response.

### Pre-Crisis Preparation

**Crisis playbook template:**

| Scenario | Likelihood | Severity | Response Lead | Escalation Path |
|----------|-----------|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Data breach | Medium | Critical | CISO + Comms | CEO within 1 hour |
| Product outage | High | High | Engineering + Comms | VP Eng within 30 min |
| Executive departure | Low | Medium | CEO + HR + Comms | Board if C-suite |
| Negative press | Medium | Medium | Comms lead | CEO if major outlet |
| Customer complaint goes viral | Medium | High | CS + Comms + Social | Dir Marketing within 1 hour |

**For each scenario, pre-draft:**
1. Initial holding statement (buy time while assessing)
2. Detailed response template (fill in specifics when crisis occurs)
3. FAQ for internal teams
4. Social media response guidelines
5. Customer communication template

### Crisis Response Protocol

**The first 60 minutes:**

| Minute | Action |
|--------|--------|
| 0-15 | Assess: What happened? What do we know? What don't we know? |
| 15-30 | Assemble: Crisis team convenes (can be virtual) |
| 30-45 | Decide: Response approach — proactive disclosure or wait for inquiry? |
| 45-60 | Communicate: Internal team first, then external (holding statement if full response not ready) |

**Response principles:**
1. **Speed over perfection** — A timely "we're aware and investigating" beats a late perfect response
2. **Transparency over spin** — Say what you know, admit what you don't
3. **Empathy first** — Acknowledge impact before explaining cause
4. **Action-oriented** — State what you're doing about it
5. **Single spokesperson** — One voice reduces contradictions

**Holding statement template:**
```
We are aware of [situation] and are actively investigating. We take this
seriously and are working to [resolve/understand/address] the situation.
We will provide an update by [specific time]. [If applicable: affected
customers have been notified with specific steps to take.]
```

**Limitations**: Crisis communication cannot fix a crisis — it can only manage perception while the underlying issue is resolved. The quality of the response matters, but the speed and substance of the actual fix matters more.

## Award and Recognition Strategy

### Pursuing Industry Awards

**When to use**: Building third-party credibility through industry recognition programs.

**Award prioritization:**

| Factor | Question |
|--------|----------|
| **Relevance** | Does our target audience see and respect this award? |
| **Feasibility** | Do we meet the criteria and have strong enough evidence? |
| **Effort** | What is the submission effort vs. potential benefit? |
| **Reusability** | Can we leverage the recognition across marketing? |

**Submission best practices:**
- Start with the evaluation criteria, not your company story
- Lead with metrics and specific outcomes
- Include customer testimonials or case studies
- Quantify impact wherever possible
- Follow word limits precisely (evaluators appreciate respect for their time)

**Limitations**: Award programs vary in credibility. Pay-to-play awards (where winning is essentially guaranteed for sponsors) have lower credibility than competitive programs with independent judging. Focus on awards that your buyers and analysts actually reference.

## Media Monitoring and Measurement

### Tracking PR Effectiveness

**When to use**: Measuring the impact of PR activities and media coverage.

**Metrics that matter:**

| Metric | What It Measures | How to Track |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Share of voice** | Your mentions vs. competitors | Media monitoring tool (Meltwater, Cision) |
| **Message pull-through** | Whether key messages appear in coverage | Manual coding of articles |
| **Tier 1 placements** | Coverage in top-tier target publications | Manual tracking + media monitoring |
| **Sentiment** | Positive/neutral/negative coverage ratio | Media monitoring + manual review |
| **Referral traffic** | Web visits from media coverage | Google Analytics UTM tracking |
| **Domain authority impact** | Backlinks from media coverage | SEO tools (Ahrefs, Moz) |

**Monthly PR report template:**

| Metric | This Month | Last Month | Trend | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------|-------|
| Total mentions | | | | |
| Tier 1 placements | | | | |
| Share of voice | | | | |
| Sentiment (pos/neg/neu) | | | | |
| Key message presence | | | | |
| Referral traffic from PR | | | | |

**Limitations**: PR measurement is inherently imperfect. Media impressions and AVE (Advertising Value Equivalency) are vanity metrics. Focus on outcome metrics: did coverage drive traffic, leads, or influence deals? The connection is often indirect and hard to attribute precisely.


## Common Pitfalls

- Media outlet requirements and editorial calendars change — verify before pitching
- Thought leadership must be evidence-based — opinions without data undermine credibility
- Press release distribution costs vary widely — don't assume pricing
